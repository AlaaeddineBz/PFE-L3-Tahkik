Abstract
Abstract
Our ﬁnal study project, Tahkik , is a mobile and web platform dedicated to Quran recita-
tion and Tajweed learning, powered by Artiﬁcial Intelligence. The platform aims to bridge
the gap between traditional Quranic education and modern technology by providing learn-
ers with instant AI-driven feedback on their recitation, while empowering teachers with
a comprehensive dashboard to review and manage student progress.
The mobile application, built with Flutter, allows users to read the Quran in the Warsh
script, record their recitation, and receive real-time pronunciation analysis. The web dash-
board, developed with React and TypeScript on top of Supabase, provides teachers and
administrators with tools for recitation review, student management, certiﬁcate (Ijaza)
issuance, subscription management, and live AI/system telemetry.
Behind the application, a containerised inference stack — a Caddy reverse proxy, a Go
API gateway, and a Python FastAPI server hosting an NVIDIA NeMo FastConformer
Hybrid CTC/RNNT model — delivers batch and streaming Arabic speech recognition. A
Tajweed (madd) analyser running on top of the CTC head grades the recitation against
the Warsh ‘an Nafî’ canonical text. To specialise the recognition engine on the Warsh
riwaya, we built a 13,024-verse three-reciter dataset and ﬁne-tuned a Whisper checkpoint,
reducing the Word Error Rate from 48.4%to25.5%on a held-out Warsh test split.
This combined report consolidates: (1) the main dissertation that describes the platform’s
mobile and web layers, (2) the technical report that documents the inference server ar-
chitecture, API contracts, and deployment, and (3) the companion AI/ML report that
describes the dataset preparation pipeline, the quality-assurance loop, the ﬁne-tuning
runs, and the WER analysis.
Keywords: Quran, Tajweed, Warsh, AI, Speech Recognition, Whisper, NeMo FastCon-
former, Flutter, React, Go, FastAPI, Supabase, Mobile Application, E-Learning, Web-
Socket Streaming, Fine-Tuning.

Résumé
Notre projet de ﬁn d’études, Tahkik , est une plateforme mobile et web dédiée à
l’apprentissage de la récitation du Coran et du Tajweed, alimentée par l’Intelligence Ar-
tiﬁcielle. La plateforme vise à combler le fossé entre l’éducation coranique traditionnelle
et la technologie moderne en fournissant aux apprenants un retour instantané généré par
l’IA sur leur récitation, tout en oﬀrant aux enseignants un tableau de bord complet pour
examiner et gérer les progrès des étudiants.
L’application mobile, développée avec Flutter, permet aux utilisateurs de lire le Coran en
écriture Warsh, d’enregistrer leur récitation et de recevoir une analyse de prononciation
en temps réel. Le tableau de bord web, développé avec React et TypeScript par-dessus
Supabase, fournit aux enseignants et aux administrateurs des outils de gestion.
Derrière l’application, une pile d’inférence containerisée (Caddy + serveur Go +
serveur Python FastAPI hébergeant un modèle NVIDIA NeMo FastConformer Hybride
CTC/RNNT ) assure la reconnaissance vocale arabe en mode batch et en streaming.
Un analyseur de Tajweed (madd) basé sur l’alignement forcé CTC évalue la récitation
par rapport au texte canonique de Warsh ‘an Nafî’. Pour spécialiser le moteur de
reconnaissance sur la riwaya Warsh, nous avons construit un jeu de données de 13 024
versets et trois récitateurs et aﬃné un modèle Whisper, réduisant le taux d’erreur de
mots (WER) de 48,4%à25,5%.
Ce rapport combiné regroupe: (1) le mémoire principal décrivant les couches mobile et
web, (2) le rapport technique du serveur d’inférence, et (3) le rapport complémentaire d’IA
décrivant la préparation des données, l’assurance qualité, les entraînements et l’analyse
des résultats.
Mots-clés: Coran, Tajweed, Warsh, IA, Reconnaissance Vocale, Whisper, NeMo Fast-
Conformer, Flutter, React, Go, FastAPI, Supabase, Application Mobile, E-Learning,
Streaming WebSocket, Aﬃnage.

General Introduction
The recitation of the Holy Quran holds a central place in Islamic practice and spirituality.
For over fourteen centuries, the science of Tajweed — the set of rules governing the correct
pronunciation of Quranic verses — has been transmitted orally from qualiﬁed scholars
to students through direct, face-to-face instruction. This traditional method, known as
Talaqqi andMushafahah , remains the gold standard for learning proper recitation.
In recent years, the rapid advancement of Artiﬁcial Intelligence (AI), particularly in the
ﬁelds of Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR) and Natural Language Processing (NLP),
has opened new possibilities for educational technology. Models such as OpenAI’s Whis-
per have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in transcribing and understanding spoken
language across multiple languages, including Arabic. These technological breakthroughs
present a unique opportunity to develop intelligent tools that can assist learners in per-
fecting their Quranic recitation.
Problem Statement
Despite the abundance of digital Quranic resources, most existing applications func-
tion merely as digital copies of the Mushaf or provide pre-recorded recitations for
passive listening. Very few platforms oﬀer interactive, real-time feedback on a user’s
own recitation quality. The shortage of qualiﬁed Tajweed teachers, especially for
non-Arabic speakers and those in remote areas, further limits access to personal-
ized instruction. Furthermore, no existing platform eﬀectively combines AI-driven
instant feedback with a structured teacher review system, leaving a signiﬁcant gap
in the Quranic e-learning ecosystem.
The main objectives of this project are:
1.To develop a cross-platform mobile application (using Flutter) that enables users to
read the Quran and record their recitation for AI-based evaluation.
2.To integrate an AI-powered speech recognition engine (Whisper ﬁne-tuned on Warsh
recitation for the training pipeline; NVIDIA NeMo FastConformer in production)
for real-time pronunciation analysis and Tajweed error detection.
3.To build a web-based administration and teacher dashboard (using React) for man-
aging students, reviewing recitations, and providing manual feedback.

4.To create a comprehensive learning ecosystem with progress tracking, practice ses-

1.2Domain Overview
1.2.1 Deﬁnition and Context
Tajweed
Tajweed literally means “to make well” or “to improve. ” In the context of Quranic
recitation, it refers to the set of linguistic and phonetic rules applied when reading
the Quran. These rules govern the correct pronunciation of each letter from its
proper articulation point ( Makhraj ) with its associated attributes ( Sifat ). Proper
application of Tajweed is considered obligatory ( Fard) upon every Muslim who
recites the Quran, as incorrect pronunciation can alter the meaning of the sacred
text.
1.2.2 Traditional Learning Methods
Traditionally, Tajweed is learned through direct interaction with a qualiﬁed teacher
(Sheikh ). This process involves two key methods:
•Talaqqi (Receiving) : The student listens to the teacher recite, absorbing the
correct pronunciation and rhythm.
4
Tahkik — AI-Powered Quran Recitation L3 Computer Science — 2025/2026
•Mushafahah (Lip-to-lip) : The student recites back to the teacher, who observes
the mouth movements and corrects pronunciation in real-time.
While highly eﬀective, this traditional method faces several modern challenges:
•Accessibility : A critical shortage of qualiﬁed teachers, especially in non-Arabic
speaking regions and remote areas.
•Time Constraints : Scheduling regular sessions with a teacher is diﬃcult for indi-
viduals with busy lifestyles.
•Scalability : A single teacher can only attend to a limited number of students
eﬀectively at any given time.
•Cost : Private tutoring can be expensive and unaﬀordable for many learners.
1.2.3 Key Concepts
Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR)
Automatic Speech Recognition is the computational task of converting spoken lan-
guage into written text. Modern ASR systems use deep learning models trained on
large datasets of audio-text pairs to achieve high accuracy across multiple languages
and dialects.
Whisper Model
Whisper is a general-purpose speech recognition model developed by OpenAI.
Trained on 680,000 hours of multilingual data, it demonstrates strong performance
in transcription, translation, and language identiﬁcation. Its support for Arabic
makes it a promising candidate for Quranic recitation analysis.

2.2System Development Process
Our development process is inspired by Agile Methodology, based on ﬂexibility, iterative
development and continuous feedback. Our workﬂow follows these practices:
Phase 1 — Backlog Management
We maintain a ﬂexible list of features and tasks. Every 10 to 15 days, we select a set
of prioritized tasks forming our Sprint Backlog, including implementation (mobile and
web), UI/UX design, AI integration, and system conception.
Phase 2 — Sprint Cycle (1–2 Weeks)
Each sprint focuses on producing a speciﬁc feature. The team plans upcoming tasks,
implements them based on each member’s role, reviews completed work, and provides
feedback for improvement.
Phase 3 — Continuous Review
We conduct continuous informal reviews where each member presents their work, and
the rest of the team provides constructive feedback on quality and direction.
Phase 4 — Incremental Production
At the end of each sprint, a part of the platform is complete and integrated, leading to
a fully functional product through incremental releases.

3.2.3 Backend API — Go
Figure 3.3: Go logo
Go(Golang) is a statically typed, compiled language developed by Google, known for its
simplicity and high concurrency performance. The Tahkik Go server acts as a lightweight
REST gateway in front of the Python inference backend: it accepts batch audio uploads on
POST /api/v1/evaluate , validates them, forwards them to the inference service, and ex-
poses a Bearer-token-gated GET /api/v1/system-health endpoint (plus its WebSocket
variant /api/v1/system-health/ws ) that the web dashboard polls for live host, con-
tainer, and inference telemetry. A public GET /health liveness probe is also provided
for Caddy and Docker healthchecks. The complementary real-time WebSocket stream-
ing path ( /ws/stream ) is served by the FastAPI inference backend directly to the mobile
client; the Go server is intentionally kept stateless and proxy-free for streaming workloads.
The full server architecture, request lifecycle, and deployment procedure are detailed in
the companion Tahkik Inference Server report.

achieving 6.55 % WER on the EveryAyah Quran benchmark. It is deployed as a
Docker container on a DigitalOcean droplet, behind the Caddy reverse proxy. For local

4. Introduction
This technical report documents the server-side infrastructure of the Tahkik project —
an AI-powered Quranic recitation analysis system specialising in the Warsh ‘an Naﬁ’
riwaya (Tariq al-Azraq). The server bridges the Flutter mobile application with the
speech-recognition model, accepts audio uploads (or live PCM streams), runs an optional
Tajweed (madd) analysis against a reference text, and returns structured transcription
and error results.
The production stack is composed of three containerised components running side-
by-side on a single Linux host: a Caddy reverse proxy (auto-TLS via Let’s
Encrypt), a Go HTTP API gateway, and a Python FastAPI inference server
backed by the NVIDIA NeMo FastConformer Hybrid CTC/Transducer model
(nvidia/stt_ar_fastconformer_hybrid_large_pcd_v1.0 ). A WebSocket stream-
ing endpoint ( /ws/stream ) delivers real-time partial transcription, and a separate
/api/v1/system-health endpoint (plus its WebSocket variant) exposes host metrics,
container status, and inference latency for the operations dashboard.
This report covers the architecture, component details, data pipeline, madd analyser, API
contracts, deployment procedure, security model, and operational concerns of the server.

•Sliding window : Each partial transcribes the last PARTIAL_WINDOW_S = 8

•Final window : On stop , the last FINAL_WINDOW_S = 60 seconds (NeMo

•Per-connection + global locks : One inference at a time inside a connec-

•Generation counter : Each reset increments a counter; in-ﬂight tasks com-

unbounded memory growth.
•Exception isolation : Errors inside partial-window tasks are logged with full
traceback and do not tear down the WebSocket connection.

7. Data Pipeline
7.1Audio Processing Flow
When an audio ﬁle arrives at the server, it passes through the following stages:
Phase 1 — Audio Reception
The Go server receives the multipart upload, validates the ﬁle extension and size (max
50 MB), reads the binary payload into memory, optionally extracts the reference_text
form ﬁeld, and forwards both to the Python backend.
Phase 2 — Audio Preprocessing
The Python backend writes the bytes to a temp ﬁle under /tmp , loads it via
librosa.load(sr=16000, mono=True) , and clamps the duration to the last 60 seconds.
Unlike Whisper, NeMo FastConformer handles long audio directly, so the legacy 30-
second chunking step is no longer required.
Phase 3 — Inference
The audio array is handed to model.transcribe(...) . When a reference_text
is present, the call is invoked with return_hypotheses=True, timestamps=True so
the wrapper can extract per-word and per-character timestamps from the returned
Hypothesis object.
Phase 4 — Optional Madd Analysis
When reference_text is provided, a separate CTC forced-alignment pass (Chapter 8)
produces per-letter timings, which are then fed into the madd analyser. The analyser
returns errors[] and an auto-calibrated haraka_ms .
Phase 5 — Result Assembly
The transcription, conﬁdence, elapsed time, and (optionally) madd errors are seri-
alised as JSON and returned to the Go server, which wraps it in the standard
{"status":"success","data":{...}} envelope.

8. Madd (Tajweed) Analysis
8.1Goal
Given a reference text the reciter is expected to say (Warsh orthography) and the au-
dio they actually produced, the madd analyser detects whether each expected vowel-
elongation ( madd ) was held for the correct number of counts ( harakat ), grading mistakes
asminor ormajor . It targets the Warsh ‘an Naﬁ’ riwaya via Tariq al-Azraq.
8.2Rules Covered
Table 8.1: Madd rules with expected counts and acceptance windows
Rule (key) Description Expected Window
tabii Natural madd (no hamza, no sukun) 2 1.5–2.7
badal Hamza precedes madd letter (qasr) 2 1.5–2.7
muttasil Madd + hamza, same word 6 5.0–7.5
munfasil Madd at end of word, hamza next 6 5.0–7.5
lazim_muthaqqal Madd + shaddah 6 5.0–7.5
lazim_mukhaffaf Madd + permanent sukun 6 5.0–7.5
aarid Madd near end of word at waqf (qasr) 2 1.5–2.7
lin Lin letter at waqf (qasr) 2 1.5–2.7
Warsh/Azraq permits multiple wujuh (2 / 4 / 6 counts) for Badal, Aarid, and Lin; the
analyser locks to the canonical qasr (2 counts) so each recitation is graded against a single
speciﬁc wajh. The acceptance window widens for the 6-count rules to absorb measurement
noise.

8.3Pipeline
Step 1 — Reference Parsing
parse_reference(text) inmadd.py walks each word character-by-character, detects
every madd letter (alif, waw, ya, dagger-alif, small waw/ya, alif-madda), classiﬁes its
hukm using vowel and post-letter context, and emits a MaddSpan carrying the expected
counts and tolerance.
Step 2 — Word Alignment
The reference words are aligned to ASR words via Levenshtein distance over diacritic-
stripped letters ( strip_diacritics also normalises alif-wasl, alif-maqsura, and ta-
marbuta to the forms the model emits). Reference words that the ASR didn’t recognise
emit a major omission error.
Step 3 — CTC Forced Alignment
madd_alignment.align_reference runs the model’s CTC head over the audio (one
extra forward pass, no RNNT cost), tokenises the trimmed reference with the model’s
SentencePiece tokenizer, and forced-aligns via torchaudio.functional.forced_align .
Output is a list of CharSpan(global_char_index, char, start, end) giving real per-
letter timings.
Step 4 — Haraka Calibration
estimate_haraka_ms computes one haraka in milliseconds from the recording itself. The
primary signal is total audio duration divided by total emitted characters (a speech-rate
proxy); two corroborating medians (all-char durations and Tabii-madd durations divided
by 2) are logged but not blended. The result is clamped to [120,600]ms.
Step 5 — Grading
For each MaddSpan , the analyser intersects its character range with the CTC spans (or
falls back to linear interpolation across the matched ASR word), measures the duration
in milliseconds, divides by haraka_ms to get observed counts, and grades against the
rule’s window: outside the window →major ; inside but |∆|>0.7from expected →
minor ; otherwise no error.
8.4Waqf Detection
A word is treated as a waqf (pause) when followed by ≥300ms of silence in the ASR
alignment, or when it is the last word in the transcript. Ayah boundaries are deliberately
72
Tahkik — AI-Powered Quran Recitation L3 Computer Science — 2025/2026
notused — only acoustic silence drives the decision. aarid andlinspans are conditional
on waqf; a word-ﬁnal madd letter emits both an aarid candidate (requires waqf) and a
tabii fallback (skip-if-waqf), and exactly one of the two ever grades.
8.5Output Schema
Each detected mistake is a TajweedError JSON object containing the word, its index,
the rule ( Al-Madd ), the Arabic sub-rule name, severity ( minor /major ), expected and
detected counts, a one-sentence explanation, and a millisecond timestamp range. The full
payload also surfaces haraka_ms so the client can display the calibrated value.

11. Troubleshooting
11.0.1 Inference Server Unreachable / 502 from Caddy
Diagnosis
Caddy is up but the Go server cannot reach the Python container. Either the Python
container is still loading the NeMo model (cold start can take 10 min on ﬁrst boot — the
wheel install plus model download) or the container has crashed.
Resolution
docker compose -f /opt/tahkik-server/docker-compose.yml logs python
–tail=50 . Watch for [inference] model ready (warmup <ms>) . If you see Python-
level tracebacks, restart with docker compose restart python .
11.0.2 Model Download Timeout
Diagnosis
First-time model download from HuggingFace can take several minutes on a slow link.
The Python server prints [inference] loading NeMo model ... and stays silent until
the checkpoint is fully fetched into /cache/nemo .
Resolution
Wait for the download to complete. Subsequent restarts use the cached weights in the
nemo_cache named volume.

Diagnosis
503means HEALTH_TOKEN is unset in the environment (the endpoint fails closed). 401

13. Scalability and Load Handling
13.1 Current Concurrency Model
The Go API server handles concurrent HTTP connections natively through goroutines.
The inference backend, however, intentionally serialises all model invocations via a module-
level asyncio.Semaphore(1) plus a per-connection asyncio.Lock on the WebSocket
path:
Concurrency Limitations
•Global inference semaphore : One inference at a time across the entire
Python process. Prevents CPU over-subscription and non-deterministic out-
put ordering. Concurrent requests are queued.
•Single-worker Uvicorn : The container runs one Uvicorn worker. Multi-
process scaling would require multiple model copies — ∼500MB of CPU
RAM each — which is infeasible on the baseline 8 GB droplet.
•Model memory footprint : The FastConformer checkpoint occupies
∼500MB of CPU RAM (or ∼1.2GB of GPU VRAM in FP16). Loading
multiple instances scales linearly.

Recommended Scalability Enhancements
•GPU upgrade : A single T4/L4 GPU droplet cuts ﬁnal latency below 2 s

•Horizontal scaling : Run multiple droplets behind a load balancer. Each

•Bounded request queue : Add a token-bucket or in-memory queue at the
Go layer to reject excess work with 503 Service Unavailable rather than
accumulating unbounded latency.
•Batching on GPU : On GPU hardware, batch multiple audio inputs in one
forward pass to amortise the ﬁxed kernel-launch overhead.

14.1 Transport Layer
Caddy terminates TLS using a Let’s Encrypt certiﬁcate issued automatically on the ﬁrst

behaviour. The Go and Python containers never bind to a public address — they expose

14.2 Input Validation
The Go API gateway enforces multiple layers of input validation before any data reaches
the inference backend:
Validation Rules
•HTTP method :/api/v1/evaluate accepts only POST ; other methods re-

•File size limit :r.ParseMultipartForm(50 « 20) enforces a strict 50 MB

past the multipart header.
•Limit-read of audio : The handler wraps the form ﬁle in
io.LimitReader(file, 50«20) so a malformed multipart cannot stream
past the declared ceiling.
•Format check at the backend : The Python /evaluate handler re-checks

15. Request Lifecycle
15.1 End-to-End Request Flow
The following describes the complete lifecycle of a single audio-evaluation request, from
client submission to response delivery.
Step 1 — Client Submission
The Flutter mobile application captures or selects an audio recording, optionally pairs it
with the ayah text the user was asked to recite, and sends it as a multipart/form-data
POST request to https://<SITE_HOST>/api/v1/evaluate . The audio is attached un-
der the audio form ﬁeld; the reference (if any) under reference_text .
Step 2 — Caddy TLS & Routing
Caddy terminates the TLS handshake, runs the CORS preﬂight if applicable,
and matches the request against the @api path matcher. The request is
reverse-proxied to go-server:8080 on the internal compose network. The CORS
Access-Control-Allow-Origin response header is set from $DASHBOARD_ORIGIN .
Step 3 — Go Server Validation
The Go server parses the multipart form (enforcing the 50 MB limit), extracts the audio
ﬁeld, validates the ﬁle extension against the whitelist, reads the binary data into memory
through a limit-reader, and captures the optional reference_text .
Step 4 — Inference Forwarding
The Go server constructs a fresh multipart/form-data request containing both audio
andreference_text (when supplied), targets $INFERENCE_SERVER_URL/evaluate , and
attaches the Authorization: Bearer $HF_TOKEN header if available. The HTTP client
uses a 5-minute timeout.
90
Tahkik — AI-Powered Quran Recitation L3 Computer Science — 2025/2026
Step 5 — Backend Audio Processing
The Python FastAPI handler receives the upload, writes it to a temp ﬁle under /tmp ,
and loads it via librosa.load(sr=16000, mono=True) . The audio array is clamped to
the last 60 seconds.
Step 6 — Model Inference
The array is passed to model.transcribe(...) under the global inference semaphore.
When reference_text is present, the call requests timestamps and hypotheses so per-
word and per-character timings can be extracted.
Step 7 — Optional Madd Analysis
The CTC head is run for forced alignment, the analyser computes per-rule errors, and
the response payload is augmented with errors[] andharaka_ms .
Step 8 — Response Assembly
The temp ﬁle is deleted in a finally block. The JSON result is returned to the Go
server, which wraps it in the standard {"status":"success","data":{...}} envelope
and forwards it back through Caddy to the Flutter client.

19. Theoretical Background
19.1 Introduction
This chapter establishes the conceptual foundations on which the rest of the report builds.
We begin with a high-level deﬁnition of Automatic Speech Recognition, present the Trans-
former architecture and the Whisper model in particular, formalise the Word Error Rate
metric used throughout the evaluation, and conclude with the Tajweed concepts and
Warsh-speciﬁc peculiarities that motivate the entire project.
19.2 Automatic Speech Recognition
Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR)
Automatic Speech Recognition is the computational task of mapping an acous-
tic signal x(a digitised audio waveform) to a sequence of written tokens y=
(y1, y2, . . . , y T). Modern ASR systems estimate the conditional distribution p(y|x)
using deep neural networks trained on large parallel corpora of audio–text pairs.
A canonical ASR pipeline comprises four stages:
1.Feature extraction : the raw waveform is converted into a compact frequency-
domain representation, most commonly a log-Mel spectrogram.
2.Acoustic modelling : a neural encoder maps the spectrogram to high-dimensional
contextual representations.
3.Decoding : an autoregressive decoder (or a CTC head) generates the most likely
token sequence given the encoded audio.
4.Post-processing : text normalisation, punctuation restoration, and domain-
speciﬁc cleanup are applied to produce the ﬁnal transcription.

19.5 Fine-Tuning by Transfer Learning
Fine-Tuning
Fine-tuning is the process of continuing the training of a pre-trained neural network
on a smaller, task-speciﬁc dataset. Because the network has already learned strong
general representations during pre-training, ﬁne-tuning typically requires orders
of magnitude less data and compute than training from scratch, while preserving
generalisation.
In this work, all parameters of the base Whisper checkpoint are updated during ﬁne-
tuning (full ﬁne-tuning, not parameter-eﬃcient adaptation). We use a small learning rate
(10−5) and a short schedule (3,000 steps) precisely to avoid catastrophic forgetting of the
general Arabic phonology already learned by the base model. Empirically, this preserves
the model’s ability to handle reciter and microphone variability while specialising it to
Warsh orthography.

19.7 Tajweed: Phonetics of Quranic Recitation
Tajweed
Tajweed ( tajwîd ) literally means “to make well” or “to improve. ” In Quranic studies,
it refers to the body of phonetic and prosodic rules that govern correct recitation.
Each rule prescribes how speciﬁc letters must be articulated in speciﬁc contexts,
and incorrect application can change the meaning of the verse.
Tajweed sits on two pillars:
•Articulation Points ( Makharij ): the precise location in the vocal apparatus
from which each Arabic phoneme is produced (throat, tongue, lips, nasal cavity,
. . . ). There are 17 traditional makhraj for the 28 Arabic letters.
•Articulation Attributes ( Sifat ): secondary properties such as voicing, intensity,
elevation, and echoing that further reﬁne the realisation of each letter.

22. Data Quality Assurance
22.1 Introduction
A permissive alignment threshold maximises recall but inevitably admits false positives.
This chapter describes the human-in-the-loop quality assurance (QA) loop that detects,
transcribes, and corrects suspicious rows, with a particular focus on Warsh-speciﬁc ortho-
graphic issues (tashkeel, rasm, and the recurring lillahi pattern).
22.2 Anomaly Detection
Phase 1 — Suspicious Row Detection
The script export_suspicious.py scans the aligned CSV for rows that match any of
a set of heuristic anomaly patterns and exports them into a dedicated suspicious.csv
for downstream review.
The heuristics include:
•Repeated Basmala : rows whose label begins with the Basmala but where the
audio does not start with a Basmala (a common artefact of automatic verse cutting).
•Surah 1, Ayah 1 anomalies : spurious matches around Al-Fatihah due to its
recurring Basmala-like preﬁx.
•Identical-text duplicates : distinct audio clips whose labels collapse to the same
normalised string.
•Low-conﬁdence alignment : rows just above the 70% threshold are ﬂagged for
manual inspection.
•Length mismatch : audio whose duration is more than 2.5 ×longer or shorter than
the expected duration for the verse’s character count.
117
Tahkik — AI-Powered Quran Recitation L3 Computer Science — 2025/2026
22.3 AI-Assisted Automatic Correction
Phase 2 — Oﬀ-the-Shelf Transcription Loop
The script auto_fix_suspicious.py runs every suspicious clip through
tarteel-ai/whisper-base-ar-quran [7] and matches the resulting transcription
against the oﬃcial Warsh reference using a 75% fuzzy threshold.
The loop is implemented in four steps:
1.Load each suspicious row from suspicious.csv .
2.Transcribe its audio with the oﬀ-the-shelf Quranic Whisper.
3.Compare the candidate transcription against the api.alquran.cloud [10] Warsh
reference for the candidate (surah, ayah).
4.If the fuzzy match is above 75%, propose the reference text as a correction; otherwise
mark the row for manual review.
This bootstrapping is conservative on purpose: even though the helper model is Hafs-
trained, it is suﬃcient to identify the correct (surah, ayah) for the audio, after which we
substitute the Warsh-correct text rather than the model’s own output.
22.4 Manual Review and Application
Phase 3 — Human-in-the-Loop Correction
Proposed corrections are reviewed manually and applied via apply_fixes.py , which
updates the dataset rows in place and records the patch in a versioned JSON for trace-
ability.
The manual review focuses on:
•Verses with recurring tajweed-marker drift.
•Cases where the audio actually contains the Basmala plus the ﬁrst verse of the surah
and must therefore be re-segmented or relabelled.
•Mis-aligned verses where the heuristic was too permissive.

25. Discussion
25.1 Introduction
This chapter steps back from the training pipeline and discusses the limitations of the
present system, the ethical considerations raised by deploying AI on sacred text, the use
cases the model unlocks, and the most promising directions for future work.
25.2 Limitations
Known limitations
•Dataset size. 13,024 verses is small compared to the scale at which Whisper
itself was trained ( 680,000hours); further accuracy gains will likely require
additional Warsh recitation data.
•Three reciters only. Although the three voices are acoustically diverse,
they remain three samples of a much wider space (women, children, regional
accents are absent).
•Word-level metric. WER is a coarse proxy for tajweed correctness;
phoneme- or rule-level metrics would be more faithful to traditional teach-
ing practice.
•Alignment errors. The 70% fuzzy threshold is permissive on purpose, and
a fraction of the residual WER is actually mis-aligned reference text rather
than acoustic errors.
•Single riwaya. The model targets Warsh exclusively; deploying for other
riwayat (Hafs, Qaloon) requires a separate training run.
25.3 Ethical Considerations
Deploying AI on the Quran requires explicit ethical care. We adopt the following principles
in the design of the system:
•Sacred-text ﬁdelity. The model must never rewrite Quranic text; the output of
the ASR is presented to the user alongside the canonical reference, never instead of
it. Any corrective feedback is anchored to the canonical text.
133
Tahkik — AI-Powered Quran Recitation L3 Computer Science — 2025/2026
•Conﬁdence-aware feedback. When the model’s conﬁdence is low (e.g. noisy
audio, unfamiliar verse) the application defers to human teachers rather than dis-
playing a potentially incorrect critique.
•Human-in-the-loop. All teacher-issued certiﬁcations ( Ijaza ) on the Tahkik plat-
form are signed by a qualiﬁed teacher; the AI never issues a certiﬁcate by itself.
•Reciter attribution. Audio used in training is sourced from everyayah.com ,
where reciter attribution is explicit and the licensing is permissive for educational
and research use.
•Privacy. User-recorded audio in the deployed app is used only to compute the
requested feedback; no recordings are retained or used for further training without
explicit consent.
25.4 Use Cases and Integration
The trained model is integrated into the Tahkik mobile application via a WebSocket-based
streaming inference service hosted on Hugging Face Spaces. The mobile app streams
16 kHz PCM chunks every ∼0.5 s and receives partial and ﬁnal transcriptions with conﬁ-
dence scores; the integration architecture is documented in the companion server report.
End users experience the model as “live” transcription with sub-second latency for short
verses.
25.5 Future Work
We list, in priority order, the most impactful extensions:
Future work
1.Phoneme-level evaluation. Move beyond WER to a tajweed-aware metric
that scores Madd duration, Ghunnah nasalisation, Qalqalah echo, and other
rule-level properties. This requires forced alignment at the phoneme level (e.g.
Wav2Vec2-based) and a tajweed-rule classiﬁer on top of the aligned phonemes.
2.Larger and more diverse dataset. Add at least three more reciters, ideally
including female and child voices, and extend to verse-level audio collected by
the Tahkik app itself.
3.Error-explanation layer. Pair the ASR with a small rule-based engine
that, given an error, surfaces the speciﬁc tajweed rule violated and a remedial
exercise.
4.Model size sweep. Compare whisper-small and whisper-medium ﬁne-
tunes against the current base variant under the same data budget.
134
Tahkik — AI-Powered Quran Recitation L3 Computer Science — 2025/2026
5.Cross-riwaya transfer. Investigate whether a single multi-riwaya check-
point can serve Warsh, Hafs, and Qaloon simultaneously, with riwaya selected
via a prompt token.
6.Quantisation and on-device inference. Distil the current checkpoint and
ship an on-device variant for oﬄine practice on mid-range Android phones.
7.Continuous learning. Use teacher overrides from the Tahkik dashboard as
a labelled feedback signal to update the model periodically (with consented
data only).

General Conclusion
The primary objective of this Final Year Project was to design, develop, and deploy a
comprehensive digital platform dedicated to the learning and perfection of Quranic recita-
tion and Tajweed rules. Through the development of Tahkik , we have demonstrated
how modern technology — speciﬁcally Artiﬁcial Intelligence, cross-platform mobile devel-
opment, and cloud-based backend services — can be leveraged to address the traditional
challenges of accessibility and personalised feedback in Islamic education.
Synthesis Across the Three Parts
The Tahkik project covers three interconnected components that together describe the
system end-to-end:
•Part I — Tahkik Mobile and Web Platform. Chapters 1to3survey the ex-
isting Quranic e-learning landscape, model the platform with UML, and document
the implementation of the Flutter mobile application and the React administra-
tor/teacher dashboard, both backed by Supabase.
•Part II — Tahkik Inference Server. The technical report walks through the
three-container Docker Compose stack (Caddy reverse proxy, Go API gateway,
Python FastAPI inference service) that exposes the AI capabilities to the mobile
client. It details the API contracts, the Madd Tajweed analyser based on CTC
forced alignment, the provision-droplet.sh deployment workﬂow, and the oper-
ational concerns (security, scalability, observability).
•Part III — AI-Powered Quranic Recitation Analysis. The companion
AI/ML report documents the Warsh dataset construction (13,024 verse-level audio–
text pairs across three reciters), the data quality-assurance loop, and the two Whis-
per ﬁne-tuning runs, culminating in a checkpoint that reduces the WER from 48.4%
to25.5%on a held-out Warsh test split.
Key Contributions of the Tahkik Project
1.A cross-platform mobile application (Flutter) providing an intuitive Quran
reading and recitation recording experience with the oﬃcial KFGQPC Warsh
font.

scriptions.
3.A dedicated React/TypeScript dashboard empowering teachers to review, cor-
rect, and certify ( Ijaza ) student recitations, with a complementary adminis-

Limitations
Despite these achievements, certain limitations remain:
•The AI model’s accuracy varies across reciters outside the training set and across
regional accents.
•The current implementation requires an internet connection for AI processing (no
on-device inference yet).
•The gamiﬁcation and social-interaction features are intentionally limited in this ﬁrst
release.
•The Madd analyser locks to the canonical qasr (2-count) wajh; multi-wajh grading
is left as future work.
•The dataset is concentrated on three male reciters; coverage of female and child
voices remains a gap.
