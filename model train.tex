\section*{Abstract}
This companion technical report documents the design, implementation, and
evaluation of the AI/ML pipeline behind the Tahkik Quranic recitation
analysis platform. We address a specific gap in modern Automatic Speech
Recognition (ASR): the lack of high-quality, recitation-specific support for
the \textit{Warsh 'an Naf{\^\i}'} riwaya, the dominant Quranic recitation
across North and West Africa. Using a transfer-learning strategy, we
fine-tune OpenAI's Whisper model (in its Quranic Arabic variant
\texttt{tarteel-ai/whisper-base-ar-quran}) on a custom-built Warsh dataset of
13{,}024 verse-level audio--text pairs sourced from three verified Warsh
reciters. The dataset preparation pipeline includes API-driven text
acquisition, multi-threaded audio download, RapidFuzz-based audio--text
alignment, an automated quality-assurance loop powered by an off-the-shelf
Quranic ASR model, and Warsh-specific orthographic corrections. Two training
runs were conducted: a single-reciter baseline and a multi-reciter run with
improved text normalisation. The multi-reciter model reduces the Word Error
Rate (WER) from $48.4\%$ to $25.5\%$, outperforming the Hafs-trained
baseline on Warsh recitation. The trained model and the curated datasets are
publicly published on the Hugging Face Hub for reproducibility, and the
trained checkpoint is integrated into the production WebSocket inference
service consumed by the Tahkik mobile application.

\textbf{Keywords:} Whisper, Fine-Tuning, Quran, Warsh Recitation, Tajweed,
Automatic Speech Recognition, Word Error Rate, Hugging Face, Transfer
Learning, Multi-Reciter Training.

\chapter*{General Introduction}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{General Introduction}
\label{ch:mt_intro}

% ── Context ──
The Holy Quran is recited daily by over a billion Muslims worldwide. Beyond
its spiritual significance, its recitation is governed by a precise set of
phonetic and prosodic rules collectively known as \textit{Tajweed}. Mastery
of these rules has historically been transmitted through direct oral
instruction (\textit{Talaqqi}), and any deviation in pronunciation can
alter the meaning of the sacred text. The advent of modern Automatic Speech
Recognition (ASR) --- particularly transformer-based models such as OpenAI's
\textbf{Whisper}~\cite{radford2022whisper} --- raises a natural question: can
these large-scale, pre-trained models be specialised to recognise Quranic
recitation with the same fidelity as a human teacher?

% ── Problem ──
\begin{warnbox}[title=Problem Statement]
Existing Quranic ASR systems --- including standard Whisper and the popular
\texttt{tarteel-ai/whisper-base-ar-quran} model --- are predominantly trained
on the \textbf{Hafs} riwaya, the recitation style most common in the Middle
East. Yet, in Algeria and across North and West Africa, the dominant
recitation is the \textbf{Warsh 'an Naf{\^\i}'} riwaya, which differs from
Hafs at both the phonetic and orthographic levels. Models trained on Hafs
systematically misrecognise Warsh recitations, producing high error rates
that make them unsuitable for educational use. There is therefore a clear
need for a Warsh-specific ASR pipeline, including the dataset, the training
procedure, and the deployable model.
\end{warnbox}

% ── Objectives ──
The objectives of this companion technical report are:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item To describe in detail the construction of a high-quality Warsh
        recitation dataset, from text and audio acquisition to alignment
        and quality assurance.
  \item To document the fine-tuning of a Whisper-based ASR model on this
        dataset, including hyper-parameter choices, training dynamics, and
        evaluation methodology.
  \item To analyse the resulting model's accuracy quantitatively (Word
        Error Rate) and qualitatively (per-surah, per-reciter, and
        substitution-pattern analysis).
  \item To compare the obtained results with relevant baselines and to
        discuss the limitations, ethical implications, and future
        extensions of this work.
\end{enumerate}

% ── Methodology ──
The methodology follows a four-stage iterative pipeline (data preparation
$\rightarrow$ quality assurance $\rightarrow$ training $\rightarrow$
evaluation), with continuous improvement based on observed errors. All
training was conducted on Google Colab Pro with NVIDIA T4/A100 GPU
acceleration; all artefacts are version-controlled and published on the
Hugging Face Hub for reproducibility.

\chapter{Theoretical Background}

\section{Introduction}
This chapter establishes the conceptual foundations on which the rest of the
report builds. We begin with a high-level definition of Automatic Speech
Recognition, present the Transformer architecture and the Whisper model in
particular, formalise the Word Error Rate metric used throughout the
evaluation, and conclude with the Tajweed concepts and Warsh-specific
peculiarities that motivate the entire project.

% ─── ASR ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Automatic Speech Recognition}
\label{sec:mt_asr}

\begin{emeraldbox}[title=Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR)]
Automatic Speech Recognition is the computational task of mapping an
acoustic signal $\mathbf{x}$ (a digitised audio waveform) to a sequence of
written tokens $\mathbf{y} = (y_1, y_2, \dots, y_T)$. Modern ASR systems
estimate the conditional distribution $p(\mathbf{y}\mid\mathbf{x})$ using
deep neural networks trained on large parallel corpora of audio--text
pairs.
\end{emeraldbox}

A canonical ASR pipeline comprises four stages:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Feature extraction}: the raw waveform is converted into a
        compact frequency-domain representation, most commonly a log-Mel
        spectrogram.
  \item \textbf{Acoustic modelling}: a neural encoder maps the spectrogram
        to high-dimensional contextual representations.
  \item \textbf{Decoding}: an autoregressive decoder (or a CTC head)
        generates the most likely token sequence given the encoded audio.
  \item \textbf{Post-processing}: text normalisation, punctuation
        restoration, and domain-specific cleanup are applied to produce the
        final transcription.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Mel-Spectrogram Features}

Whisper operates on \emph{log-Mel spectrograms}, a perceptually-motivated
time--frequency representation. The waveform is windowed into 25\,ms frames
with a 10\,ms hop, the magnitude of the short-time Fourier transform is
warped through 80 Mel filters spanning 0--8\,kHz, and the result is
log-compressed:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:mt_logmel}
S_{\mathrm{mel}}(t,m) = \log\!\Big( \sum_{f} |X(t,f)|^2 \, H_m(f) + \epsilon \Big),
\end{equation}
where $X(t,f)$ is the STFT, $H_m(f)$ is the $m$-th Mel filter, and
$\epsilon$ avoids $\log 0$. For 30\,s clips at 16\,kHz this yields a
$80\times 3000$ matrix that is the input to the encoder.

% ─── Transformer ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{The Transformer Architecture}
\label{sec:mt_transformer}

The \textbf{Transformer}~\cite{vaswani2017attention} is the deep-learning
architecture underlying Whisper and most state-of-the-art ASR models. It
replaces recurrence with self-attention, allowing all positions in a
sequence to attend to each other in parallel. Given queries $Q$, keys $K$,
and values $V$, scaled dot-product attention is defined as:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:mt_attention}
\mathrm{Attention}(Q,K,V) = \mathrm{softmax}\!\left(\frac{QK^{\top}}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right) V.
\end{equation}
Multi-head attention runs $h$ such projections in parallel, concatenates
their outputs, and applies a final linear layer. A Transformer block
combines multi-head attention with a position-wise feed-forward network,
residual connections, and layer normalisation. Stacks of $N$ such blocks
form the encoder and the decoder.

% ─── Whisper ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{The Whisper Model}
\label{sec:mt_whisper}

\begin{emeraldbox}[title=Whisper (OpenAI, 2022)]
Whisper~\cite{radford2022whisper} is a family of Transformer-based
encoder--decoder ASR models trained on $680{,}000$ hours of weakly
supervised multilingual audio collected from the web. Whisper supports
transcription, translation, and language identification across 99
languages and is released under the MIT licence. Five sizes are publicly
available (\texttt{tiny}, \texttt{base}, \texttt{small}, \texttt{medium},
\texttt{large}); for this work we fine-tune the \texttt{base} variant
specialised for Quranic Arabic.
\end{emeraldbox}

\subsection{Architecture}

Whisper follows a conventional encoder--decoder layout. The
log-Mel spectrogram is first passed through two 1-D convolutional layers
that act as a learnable patch tokeniser; sinusoidal positional embeddings
are then added and the result is fed through $N_{\mathrm{enc}}$ encoder
blocks. The decoder is autoregressive: it takes the previously generated
text tokens (with learned positional embeddings) plus the encoder output as
inputs and predicts the next token via a softmax over a 51{,}865-token
multilingual BPE vocabulary. Special prompt tokens at the start of the
decoder sequence select the task (transcribe vs.\ translate), the language,
and whether timestamps should be emitted.

\subsection{Why Whisper for Quranic ASR?}

Three properties make Whisper a strong starting point for Quranic
recitation analysis:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Multilingual pre-training}: Whisper has already seen
        substantial Arabic data, dramatically reducing the amount of
        Quranic data we need to fine-tune it.
  \item \textbf{Robustness to noise}: training on weakly supervised
        web-scale audio makes Whisper resilient to background noise,
        reverberation, and microphone variability --- conditions that
        learners' phone microphones invariably exhibit.
  \item \textbf{Open ecosystem}: the Hugging Face \texttt{transformers}
        library provides ready-made fine-tuning recipes and the Hub
        provides free model and dataset hosting.
\end{itemize}

% ─── Fine-Tuning ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Fine-Tuning by Transfer Learning}
\label{sec:mt_finetune}

\begin{emeraldbox}[title=Fine-Tuning]
Fine-tuning is the process of continuing the training of a pre-trained
neural network on a smaller, task-specific dataset. Because the network
has already learned strong general representations during pre-training,
fine-tuning typically requires orders of magnitude less data and compute
than training from scratch, while preserving generalisation.
\end{emeraldbox}

In this work, all parameters of the base Whisper checkpoint are updated
during fine-tuning (full fine-tuning, not parameter-efficient adaptation).
We use a small learning rate ($10^{-5}$) and a short schedule (3{,}000
steps) precisely to avoid \emph{catastrophic forgetting} of the general
Arabic phonology already learned by the base model. Empirically, this
preserves the model's ability to handle reciter and microphone variability
while specialising it to Warsh orthography.

% ─── WER ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Word Error Rate (WER)}
\label{sec:mt_wer}

The standard ASR evaluation metric is the Word Error Rate, defined as the
edit distance between the hypothesis transcription $\hat{\mathbf{y}}$ and
the reference $\mathbf{y}$, normalised by the length of the reference:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:mt_wer}
\mathrm{WER} = \frac{S + D + I}{N},
\end{equation}
where $S$, $D$, and $I$ denote substitutions, deletions, and insertions
respectively, and $N$ is the number of words in the reference.

\begin{itemize}
  \item Lower is better; a WER of $0\%$ is a perfect transcription.
  \item Although WER above $100\%$ is theoretically possible (when many
        words are inserted), in practice it stays in $[0,1]$.
  \item For Quranic recitation, an effective ``word'' is a single
        whitespace-separated unit after diacritic normalisation; therefore
        a WER of $25\%$ corresponds to roughly one word in four being
        misrecognised, deleted, or inserted.
\end{itemize}

% ─── Tajweed ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Tajweed: Phonetics of Quranic Recitation}
\label{sec:mt_tajweed}

\begin{emeraldbox}[title=Tajweed]
Tajweed (\textit{tajw{\^\i}d}) literally means ``to make well'' or ``to
improve.'' In Quranic studies, it refers to the body of phonetic and
prosodic rules that govern correct recitation. Each rule prescribes how
specific letters must be articulated in specific contexts, and incorrect
application can change the meaning of the verse.
\end{emeraldbox}

Tajweed sits on two pillars:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Articulation Points (\textit{Makharij})}: the precise
        location in the vocal apparatus from which each Arabic phoneme is
        produced (throat, tongue, lips, nasal cavity, \dots). There are
        17 traditional makhraj for the 28 Arabic letters.
  \item \textbf{Articulation Attributes (\textit{Sifat})}: secondary
        properties such as voicing, intensity, elevation, and
        echoing that further refine the realisation of each letter.
\end{itemize}

\section{Quranic Recitations: Warsh vs.\ Hafs}
\label{sec:mt_warsh_vs_hafs}

The Quran has been transmitted through ten canonical readings
(\textit{Qira'at}); each reading admits multiple narrations
(\textit{Riwayat}). Two narrations dominate the modern world:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Hafs 'an 'Asim} --- the most widely printed and recited
        riwaya. It is the default of the standard Mushaf used in the
        Middle East, South Asia, and most digital Quran applications.
  \item \textbf{Warsh 'an Naf{\^\i}'} --- the dominant riwaya across North
        Africa (including Algeria), West Africa, and parts of Andalusia.
        It differs from Hafs at multiple levels.
\end{itemize}

\begin{table}[H]
  \centering
  \caption{Selected differences between the Warsh and Hafs riwayat
  (illustrative; the actual catalogue is much larger).}
  \label{tab:mt_warsh_vs_hafs}
  \renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.3}
  \begin{tabular}{lp{4.5cm}p{4.5cm}}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Aspect} & \textbf{Warsh} & \textbf{Hafs} \\
    \midrule
    Madd lengths & 6 counts in many contexts & 4 or 2 counts \\
    Hamzah & Often softened or transferred (Naql) & Pronounced fully \\
    Ra' & Tarq{\^\i}q (light) more frequent & Tafkh{\^\i}m (heavy) more frequent \\
    Imalah & Present in select words (e.g.\ \textit{majraha}) & Absent \\
    Tashk{\^\i}l density & Different tashkeel marks at several positions & Standard tashkeel \\

    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

\textbf{Why this matters for ASR.} A Whisper variant trained predominantly
on Hafs recitations has internalised a strong prior over the Hafs spelling
and acoustic distribution. When fed a Warsh recitation, the decoder
systematically biases its output toward Hafs orthography, producing
substitutions even when the audio is acoustically clear. This is precisely
the problem our fine-tuning addresses.

% ─── Quranic ASR Challenges ────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Challenges of Quranic ASR}
\label{sec:mt_quran_asr_challenges}

Beyond the Warsh--Hafs split, Quranic ASR poses several domain-specific
challenges:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Dense vocalisation.} Quranic text is fully vowelled with
        tashkeel and tajweed markers, whereas modern Arabic is usually
        unvowelled. This shifts the input distribution far from the
        Whisper pre-training corpus.
  \item \textbf{Special symbols.} Pause marks (\textit{Waqf}), elongation
        signs, and Quranic-specific glyphs (Alef Khanjariyah, small Yaa,
        \dots) appear in the reference text but not in everyday Arabic.
  \item \textbf{Silent letters.} Several letters are written but not
        pronounced (e.g.\ extra Alefs); the model must learn to
        consistently delete them at the text level.
  \item \textbf{Highly controlled prosody.} Quranic recitation is rule-bound,
        with mandatory elongations and pauses; the acoustic distribution
        is therefore narrower than spontaneous speech.
  \item \textbf{Limited supervised data.} Per-verse aligned audio--text
        pairs are scarce, especially for non-Hafs riwayat.
\end{itemize}

\section{Phase 1 --- Warsh Text Acquisition}

\phaseblock{1}{Download Warsh Quran Text}{The script
\texttt{warsh\_quran\_alignment.py} fetches the complete Warsh Quran text
from the \texttt{fawazahmed0/quran-api}~\cite{fawazapi} via verse-level
HTTP requests. Responses are cached as structured JSON keyed by surah and
ayah identifiers.}

Implementation details:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Iterates over all 114 surahs and their 6{,}236 verses.
  \item Implements exponential back-off on HTTP 429 / 5xx responses.
  \item Persists the raw JSON to disk so subsequent phases can be replayed
        without re-downloading.
  \item Computes a per-verse \emph{fingerprint} (a normalised, diacritic-
        stripped signature) used in Phase 3 for fast fuzzy matching.
\end{itemize}

\section{Phase 2 --- Audio Acquisition}

\phaseblock{2}{Multi-reciter MP3 Download}{The notebook
\texttt{tahkik\_dataset\_v2\_01\_download.ipynb} downloads MP3 recordings
from \texttt{everyayah.com}~\cite{everyayah} for three verified Warsh
reciters: Abdul Basit, Ibrahim Al-Dosary, and Yassin Al-Jazaery.}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Reciters chosen} for acoustic diversity: a slow,
        traditional voice (Abdul Basit), a contemporary moderately-paced
        voice (Al-Dosary), and an Algerian/North-African voice
        (Al-Jazaery).
  \item \textbf{Multi-threaded downloader} with bounded concurrency
        (16 workers) downloads $\sim$6{,}236 verses per reciter.
  \item Files are stored at \texttt{audio/<reciter>/<surah>\_<ayah>.mp3}
        with a flat, predictable naming scheme that simplifies alignment.
  \item Total raw audio size: $\sim$2.5\,GB across the three reciters.
\end{itemize}

\section{Phase 3 --- Audio--Text Alignment}

\phaseblock{3}{Fuzzy Audio--Text Alignment}{The core alignment step is
performed by \texttt{warsh\_quran\_alignment.py} using
RapidFuzz~\cite{rapidfuzz} with a $70\%$ similarity threshold over
verse-level fingerprints.}

The procedure is:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Load every audio file and parse its surah/ayah identifier from the
        filename.
  \item Look up the corresponding Warsh text by surah/ayah from Phase~1.
  \item Compute a fuzzy match score between the candidate text and the
        verse fingerprints in a sliding window around the expected
        location.
  \item If the score is above $70\%$, emit a row
        \texttt{(audio\_path, text, reciter, surah, ayah, score)} into the
        aligned CSV; otherwise, the row is flagged as suspicious and
        deferred to the QA loop (Chapter~\ref{ch:mt_qa}).
\end{enumerate}

This deliberately permissive threshold favours \emph{recall} at the
alignment stage; the subsequent QA loop trims false positives.

\section{Phase 4 --- Normalisation and Splitting}

\phaseblock{4}{Pre-processing for Whisper}{The
\texttt{prepare\_dataset.py} script and the notebook
\texttt{tahkik\_dataset\_v2\_03\_preprocess.ipynb} produce the final
training-ready dataset.}

The normalisation steps are:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Strip optional Tajweed-only diacritics from the labels (the model
        learns the underlying phonemes; Tajweed diacritics are a
        deterministic post-processing).
  \item Resample all audio to 16\,kHz mono PCM (Whisper's native input
        sampling rate).
  \item Truncate audio to 30\,s; pad shorter clips to a fixed feature
        length on the fly during training.
  \item Apply a 90\% / 10\% \emph{stratified} train/test split, ensuring
        every reciter is represented in both partitions.
\end{itemize}

\chapter{Data Quality Assurance}
\label{ch:mt_qa}

\section{Introduction}
A permissive alignment threshold maximises recall but inevitably admits
false positives. This chapter describes the human-in-the-loop quality
assurance (QA) loop that detects, transcribes, and corrects suspicious
rows, with a particular focus on Warsh-specific orthographic issues
(tashkeel, rasm, and the recurring \emph{lillahi} pattern).

\section{Anomaly Detection}

\phaseblock{1}{Suspicious Row Detection}{The script
\texttt{export\_suspicious.py} scans the aligned CSV for rows that match
any of a set of heuristic anomaly patterns and exports them into a
dedicated \texttt{suspicious.csv} for downstream review.}

The heuristics include:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Repeated Basmala}: rows whose label begins with the
        Basmala but where the audio does not start with a Basmala (a
        common artefact of automatic verse cutting).
  \item \textbf{Surah 1, Ayah 1 anomalies}: spurious matches around
        Al-Fatihah due to its recurring Basmala-like prefix.
  \item \textbf{Identical-text duplicates}: distinct audio clips whose
        labels collapse to the same normalised string.
  \item \textbf{Low-confidence alignment}: rows just above the 70\%
        threshold are flagged for manual inspection.
  \item \textbf{Length mismatch}: audio whose duration is more than
        2.5$\times$ longer or shorter than the expected duration for the
        verse's character count.
\end{itemize}

\section{AI-Assisted Automatic Correction}

\phaseblock{2}{Off-the-Shelf Transcription Loop}{The script
\texttt{auto\_fix\_suspicious.py} runs every suspicious clip through
\texttt{tarteel-ai/whisper-base-ar-quran}~\cite{tarteelmodel} and matches
the resulting transcription against the official Warsh reference using a
75\% fuzzy threshold.}

The loop is implemented in four steps:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Load each suspicious row from \texttt{suspicious.csv}.
  \item Transcribe its audio with the off-the-shelf Quranic Whisper.
  \item Compare the candidate transcription against the
        \texttt{api.alquran.cloud}~\cite{alqurancloud} Warsh reference
        for the candidate (surah, ayah).
  \item If the fuzzy match is above 75\%, propose the reference text as a
        correction; otherwise mark the row for manual review.
\end{enumerate}

This bootstrapping is conservative on purpose: even though the helper
model is Hafs-trained, it is sufficient to identify the correct
(surah, ayah) for the audio, after which we substitute the
\emph{Warsh-correct} text rather than the model's own output.

\section{Manual Review and Application}

\phaseblock{3}{Human-in-the-Loop Correction}{Proposed corrections are
reviewed manually and applied via \texttt{apply\_fixes.py}, which
updates the dataset rows in place and records the patch in a versioned
JSON for traceability.}

The manual review focuses on:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Verses with recurring tajweed-marker drift.
  \item Cases where the audio actually contains the Basmala plus the
        first verse of the surah and must therefore be re-segmented or
        relabelled.
  \item Mis-aligned verses where the heuristic was too permissive.
\end{itemize}

\section{Warsh-Specific Orthographic Fixes}

A significant fraction of the residual errors are not alignment errors
but \emph{orthographic} differences between the various Warsh sources.
We address these with a small family of targeted scripts.

\subsection{Tashkeel Completion}
\phaseblock{4}{Diacritic Enhancement}{The script
\texttt{fix\_warsh\_tashkeel.py} compares the aligned text to the
official Warsh reference from \texttt{api.alquran.cloud} and adds
missing tashkeel marks where the underlying letters match.}

\subsection{Rasm Normalisation}
\phaseblock{5}{Alef-Maqsura / Yaa Fixes}{The script
\texttt{fix\_warsh\_rasm.py} normalises Alef-Maqsura
(\textit{alif maqs{\^u}rah}) versus Yaa (\textit{y{\^a}'})
inconsistencies that occur because of differences between Hafs and Warsh
rasm.}

A dedicated JSON file (\texttt{warsh\_alef\_maqsura\_fixes.json}) tracks
the surah/ayah positions where this fix is required so the change is
fully auditable.

\subsection{The \textit{lillahi} Fix}
\phaseblock{6}{Special Case --- ``\textit{li-ll{\^a}hi}''}{The script
\texttt{fix\_lillahi.py} corrects the recurring missing-Shadda /
missing-Fatha pattern in the Warsh spelling of the word
\emph{lillahi} (``for Allah'').}

This pattern shows up in dozens of verses and produces a systematic
substitution at the token level if left unfixed; addressing it directly
eliminates a long tail of WER contributions.

\section{Dataset Versioning on the Hugging Face Hub}

\phaseblock{7}{Hub Publication}{The script
\texttt{cleanup\_huggingface.py} updates dataset metadata (description,
tags, splits) and pushes the resulting datasets to the Hub.}

Three dataset variants are published:

\section{Conclusion}

The quality-assurance loop converts a permissively aligned 90\%-correct
dataset into a high-precision corpus suitable for fine-tuning. By
combining heuristic anomaly detection, AI-assisted correction, and
Warsh-specific orthographic fixes, we trade a modest amount of human
review time for a substantial reduction in label noise --- a reduction
that directly translates into the WER gains reported in
Chapter~\ref{ch:mt_results}.

\section{Best-Checkpoint Selection}

The \texttt{Trainer} is configured to keep the checkpoint with the
lowest validation WER (\texttt{load\_best\_model\_at\_end=True},
\texttt{metric\_for\_best\_model="wer"}, \texttt{greater\_is\_better=False}).
For Run~2, this corresponds to the checkpoint at step $3{,}000$, which
also minimises the validation loss. There is no symptomatic divergence
between training and validation losses, suggesting that overfitting is
limited under the current data and step budget.

\section{Model Deployment}

The final checkpoint is uploaded to the Hugging Face Hub via
\texttt{model.push\_to\_hub()} as \texttt{benhadjermed/tahkik-basic-warsh}.
A smaller distilled variant (\texttt{benhadjermed/tahkik-small-warsh}) is
also published for inference scenarios that require lower latency.
These checkpoints are consumed by the production inference service that
backs the Tahkik mobile application; the inference architecture and
WebSocket streaming protocol are documented in the companion server
report.

\section{Conclusion}
In this chapter we presented the practical training procedure: a
FP16 fine-tune of \texttt{tarteel-ai/whisper-base-ar-quran} for
3{,}000 steps on a multi-reciter Warsh dataset, evaluated every 250
steps and selected on best validation WER. The next chapter analyses
the quantitative and qualitative results of this procedure.

  \item Reciters generally articulate them more carefully, reducing

\end{enumerate}

\section{Common Substitution Patterns}

A manual inspection of $200$ random Run~2 errors yields the following
recurring substitution categories:

\begin{table}[H]
  \centering
  \caption{Most frequent substitution patterns in Run~2 errors.}
  \label{tab:mt_substitutions}
  \renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.3}
  \begin{tabular}{lp{8.5cm}}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Pattern} & \textbf{Description and example} \\
    \midrule
    Tashkeel slip          & Correct letters, but the diacritic is replaced
                              by a Hafs-style equivalent. The word identity
                              is preserved; the WER penalty is one token. \\
    Alef-Maqsura / Yaa     & The verse-final Alef-Maqsura
                              (\textit{alif maqs{\^u}rah}) is rendered as
                              Yaa (\textit{y{\^a}'}), or vice versa.
                              Common in verse endings. \\
    Hamzah handling        & In Warsh the Hamzah is often softened or
                              transferred (Naql); the model occasionally
                              outputs the Hafs-style hard Hamzah instead. \\
    Madd length            & The textual form is identical, but in Warsh
                              the elongation lasts longer. Acoustic-only
                              errors that do not affect WER directly. \\
    Reciter-specific       & Two of the three reciters occasionally insert
                              short recitation markers (e.g.\ taking a
                              breath) that the model interprets as
                              vocabulary tokens. \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

These patterns suggest that the residual WER is dominated by
\emph{label-noise} rather than fundamental acoustic confusion --- a
favourable starting point for further improvements through better
text normalisation rather than larger models.

    \texttt{tarteel-ai/whisper-base-ar-quran}   & Hafs Quranic         & Hafs         & $30$--$35\%$ (reported on Hafs; higher on Warsh) \\
    \textbf{This work (\texttt{tahkik-basic-warsh})} & Warsh aligned    & Warsh        & $\mathbf{25.52\%}$ \\

  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

\textbf{Caveats.} The numbers in
Table~\ref{tab:mt_related} are not directly comparable, because each
system is evaluated on a different test set with different normalisation
conventions. The general trend, however, is unambiguous: a
Warsh-specific fine-tune outperforms generic Arabic and Hafs-trained
models on Warsh recitation by a large margin.

\section{Generalisation and Overfitting}

Three observations argue against overfitting:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item The validation WER improves monotonically alongside the
        training loss; there is no symptomatic divergence
        (Figures~\ref{fig:mt_run2_loss} and \ref{fig:mt_run2_wer}).
  \item The per-reciter spread is below 2 percentage points
        (Table~\ref{tab:mt_per_reciter}).
  \item Qualitative testing on previously unseen Warsh audio (a fourth
        reciter not used during training) shows usable transcription
        quality, with WER in the same regime as the held-out set.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Reproducibility}

The full pipeline is reproducible end-to-end:

\begin{itemize}
  \item All datasets are public on the Hub
        (\texttt{benhadjermed/warsh\_quran\_aligned},
        \texttt{...\_processed},
        \texttt{warsh-quran-multi-reciter}).
  \item All trained models are public on the Hub
        (\texttt{benhadjermed/tahkik-basic-warsh},
        \texttt{benhadjermed/tahkik-small-warsh}).
  \item All training notebooks
        (\texttt{tahkik\_01\_prepare\_dataset.ipynb},
        \texttt{tahkik\_02\_train.ipynb},
        \texttt{tahkik\_basic\_warsh.ipynb},
        \texttt{tahkik\_small\_warsh\_train.ipynb}) are checked in.
  \item Hyper-parameters and the random seed (42) are listed in
        Table~\ref{tab:mt_hyperparams}.
\end{itemize}

A clean rerun on a fresh Colab GPU will produce a model within
$\pm 0.5$\,pp WER of the reported numbers.

\section{Conclusion}
The fine-tuned Warsh ASR model significantly outperforms the closest
publicly available baselines, generalises well across reciters, and
exhibits a residual error structure dominated by label-noise rather
than acoustic confusion. The next chapter discusses the system's
limitations, ethical implications, and the most promising directions
for future work.

\chapter{Discussion}
\label{ch:mt_discussion}

\section{Introduction}
This chapter steps back from the training pipeline and discusses the
limitations of the present system, the ethical considerations raised by
deploying AI on sacred text, the use cases the model unlocks, and the
most promising directions for future work.

\section{Limitations}

\begin{warnbox}[title=Known limitations]
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Dataset size.} 13{,}024 verses is small compared to the
        scale at which Whisper itself was trained ($680{,}000$ hours);
        further accuracy gains will likely require additional Warsh
        recitation data.
  \item \textbf{Three reciters only.} Although the three voices are
        acoustically diverse, they remain three samples of a much wider
        space (women, children, regional accents are absent).
  \item \textbf{Word-level metric.} WER is a coarse proxy for tajweed
        correctness; phoneme- or rule-level metrics would be more
        faithful to traditional teaching practice.
  \item \textbf{Alignment errors.} The 70\% fuzzy threshold is
        permissive on purpose, and a fraction of the residual WER is
        actually mis-aligned reference text rather than acoustic
        errors.
  \item \textbf{Single riwaya.} The model targets Warsh exclusively;
        deploying for other riwayat (Hafs, Qaloon) requires a separate
        training run.
\end{itemize}
\end{warnbox}

\section{Ethical Considerations}

Deploying AI on the Quran requires explicit ethical care. We adopt the
following principles in the design of the system:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Sacred-text fidelity.} The model must never \emph{rewrite}
        Quranic text; the output of the ASR is presented to the user
        \emph{alongside} the canonical reference, never instead of it.
        Any corrective feedback is anchored to the canonical text.
  \item \textbf{Confidence-aware feedback.} When the model's confidence
        is low (e.g.\ noisy audio, unfamiliar verse) the application
        defers to human teachers rather than displaying a potentially
        incorrect critique.
  \item \textbf{Human-in-the-loop.} All teacher-issued certifications
        (\textit{Ijaza}) on the Tahkik platform are signed by a
        qualified teacher; the AI never issues a certificate by itself.
  \item \textbf{Reciter attribution.} Audio used in training is sourced
        from \texttt{everyayah.com}, where reciter attribution is
        explicit and the licensing is permissive for educational and
        research use.
  \item \textbf{Privacy.} User-recorded audio in the deployed app is
        used only to compute the requested feedback; no recordings are
        retained or used for further training without explicit consent.
\end{itemize}

\section{Use Cases and Integration}

The trained model is integrated into the Tahkik mobile application via a
WebSocket-based streaming inference service hosted on Hugging Face
Spaces. The mobile app streams 16\,kHz PCM chunks every $\sim$0.5\,s
and receives partial and final transcriptions with confidence scores;
the integration architecture is documented in the companion server
report. End users experience the model as ``live'' transcription with
sub-second latency for short verses.

\section{Future Work}

We list, in priority order, the most impactful extensions:

\begin{goldbox}[title=Future work]
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Phoneme-level evaluation.} Move beyond WER to a
        tajweed-aware metric that scores Madd duration, Ghunnah
        nasalisation, Qalqalah echo, and other rule-level properties.
        This requires forced alignment at the phoneme level (e.g.\
        Wav2Vec2-based) and a tajweed-rule classifier on top of the
        aligned phonemes.
  \item \textbf{Larger and more diverse dataset.} Add at least three
        more reciters, ideally including female and child voices, and
        extend to verse-level audio collected by the Tahkik app itself.
  \item \textbf{Error-explanation layer.} Pair the ASR with a small
        rule-based engine that, given an error, surfaces the specific
        tajweed rule violated and a remedial exercise.
  \item \textbf{Model size sweep.} Compare \texttt{whisper-small} and
        \texttt{whisper-medium} fine-tunes against the current
        \texttt{base} variant under the same data budget.
  \item \textbf{Cross-riwaya transfer.} Investigate whether a single
        multi-riwaya checkpoint can serve Warsh, Hafs, and Qaloon
        simultaneously, with riwaya selected via a prompt token.
  \item \textbf{Quantisation and on-device inference.} Distil the
        current checkpoint and ship an on-device variant for offline
        practice on mid-range Android phones.
  \item \textbf{Continuous learning.} Use teacher overrides from the
        Tahkik dashboard as a labelled feedback signal to update the
        model periodically (with consented data only).
\end{enumerate}
\end{goldbox}

\chapter*{General Conclusion}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{General Conclusion}
\label{ch:mt_conclusion}

This companion technical report has documented the AI/ML pipeline
behind the recitation analysis feature of the Tahkik platform. We
addressed a concrete and previously under-served gap in modern
Automatic Speech Recognition: the lack of high-quality Warsh
recitation support. To do so, we built and published a dedicated
13{,}024-verse, three-reciter Warsh dataset; we engineered a
quality-assurance loop that combines anomaly detection, AI-assisted
correction, and Warsh-specific orthographic fixes; we fine-tuned a
Quranic Whisper checkpoint twice (single- and multi-reciter); and we
analysed the resulting model both quantitatively (WER, per-reciter and
per-surah breakdown) and qualitatively (substitution patterns).

\begin{emeraldbox}[title=Key Contributions]
\begin{enumerate}
  \item A reproducible Warsh dataset preparation pipeline, fully
        scripted, with three published datasets on the Hugging Face Hub.
  \item Two trained Whisper-based ASR checkpoints
        (\texttt{tahkik-basic-warsh}, \texttt{tahkik-small-warsh})
        publicly available on the Hub, with the larger variant
        achieving $25.52\%$ WER on a held-out Warsh test split.
  \item An automated, human-in-the-loop quality assurance procedure
        targeted specifically at Warsh orthographic peculiarities.
  \item A documented WER reduction from $48.4\%$ to $25.5\%$ through a
        combination of multi-reciter training and Warsh-aware
        normalisation.
  \item A clean integration into the production Tahkik mobile
        application through a streaming WebSocket inference service.
\end{enumerate}
\end{emeraldbox}

This work demonstrates that targeted fine-tuning of a strong
multilingual ASR baseline can deliver real, measurable quality gains
for an under-served religious recitation tradition, while remaining
fully reproducible and open. The contributions of this companion report
support and extend the main Tahkik platform described in the main
dissertation.

\begin{emeraldbox}[title=Closing Statement]
\begin{center}
\large\itshape
``And recite the Quran with measured recitation.''\\[6pt]
\normalsize\normalfont\color{black} --- Surah Al-Muzzammil, Verse 4
\end{center}
\vspace{6pt}
\normalsize
By specialising modern speech recognition on the Warsh tradition, this
work contributes a small but concrete step towards making rigorous,
personalised Tajweed feedback accessible to every reciter, regardless
of their access to a qualified teacher.
\end{emeraldbox}
